## Overview

AI agents are autonomous software programs that perceive their environment, make decisions, and take actions to achieve specific goals without continuous human intervention. Unlike traditional software that follows rigid, pre-defined instructions, AI agents leverage large language models and reasoning capabilities to adapt their behavior based on context and experience. They represent a shift from passive tools that wait for commands to active systems that can plan, reason, and execute tasks independently.

The concept draws from classical [[intelligent agents]] in artificial intelligence, but modern AI agents are distinguished by their ability to use natural language as a primary interface and to handle ambiguous, real-world tasks. They can break down complex objectives into sub-tasks, use tools and APIs, and iteratively refine their approach based on feedback. This makes them particularly valuable for automating knowledge work, conducting research, and managing multi-step workflows.

## Components

AI agents are typically built around three core components that form a perception-reasoning-action loop.

**Perception** refers to the agent's ability to gather information about its environment. This may include parsing text input, reading files, querying databases, accessing web content, or receiving signals from integrated systems. In many implementations, the perception layer also includes memory systems that allow the agent to retain context across interactions.

**Reasoning** is the cognitive engine of the agent. It involves interpreting perception inputs, evaluating options, planning steps, and deciding on actions to take. Modern AI agents use large language models to perform reasoning, often augmented with structured prompting techniques such as chain-of-thought reasoning. This component enables the agent to handle novel situations and make judgments under uncertainty.

**Action** encompasses everything the agent does to affect its environment. This ranges from generating text responses and calling APIs to executing code, modifying files, or triggering external processes. Effective agents are designed with careful consideration of action space—the set of possible actions they can take—and how those actions translate into measurable outcomes.

## Types

AI agents can be classified based on their architecture and operational scope.

**Single-agent systems** consist of one agent working independently to accomplish a task. The agent may have access to various tools and memory systems, but operates without direct communication or collaboration with other agents. Single-agent designs are common in personal assistants, automated research tools, and task-specific applications.

**Multi-agent systems** involve multiple agents working together, either cooperatively or competitively. In cooperative multi-agent setups, agents divide labor, specialize in different subtasks, and share information to achieve a common objective. This approach can improve scalability and allow complex problems to be solved through collaboration. Multi-agent architectures are particularly relevant in domains like distributed problem solving, simulations, and complex workflow automation.

- **Rich** for banner/panels, **prompt_toolkit** for input with autocomplete
- **KawaiiSpinner** (`agent/display.py`) — animated faces during API calls, `┊` activity feed for tool results

- Skill slash commands: `agent/skill_commands.py` scans `~/.hermes/skills/`, injects as **user message** (not system prompt) to preserve prompt caching

### Slash Command Registry (`hermes_cli/commands.py`)

All slash commands are defined in a central `COMMAND_REGISTRY` list of `CommandDef` objects. Every downstream consumer derives from this registry automatically:

- **CLI** — `process_command()` resolves aliases via `resolve_command()`, dispatches on canonical name
- **Gateway** — `GATEWAY_KNOWN_COMMANDS` frozenset for hook emission, `resolve_command()` for dispatch
- **Gateway help** — `gateway_help_lines()` generates `/help` output
- **Telegram** — `telegram_bot_commands()` generates the BotCommand menu
- **Slack** — `slack_subcommand_map()` generates `/hermes` subcommand routing
- **Autocomplete** — `COMMANDS` flat dict feeds `SlashCommandCompleter`
- **CLI help** — `COMMANDS_BY_CATEGORY` dict feeds `show_help()`
