# Jobs To Be Done

## Overview

Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) is a theory of customer behavior and a product development framework that reframes how we think about what customers really "buy." Instead of focusing on the demographic characteristics of customers or the features of a product, JTBD focuses on the underlying progress that customers are trying to make in their lives—the "job" they are hiring a product or service to do. The classic formulation, popularized by Clayton Christensen at Harvard Business School, is: "People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole."

The JTBD framework emerged from Christensen's research into why customers adopt or abandon products, particularly in the context of his theory of disruptive innovation. The key insight was that customers "hire" products to accomplish specific functional, emotional, or social jobs, and they "fire" products when those products fail to do the job adequately—or when a better alternative emerges. Understanding the job, not the product, is what enables companies to build things people truly want and to identify opportunities for innovation that competitors have missed.

JTBD has been adopted broadly across technology companies, consumer products, healthcare, and B2B services. It provides a language and methodology for deep customer understanding that cuts through the noise of feature requests and survey data.

## Key Concepts

### The Job Statement

A well-formed job statement follows a specific structure:

```
When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].
```

- **Situation**: The context or circumstance that triggers the need
- **Motivation**: What the person is trying to achieve or accomplish
- **Expected outcome**: The tangible result or state they're working toward

For example:
- "When I'm running late to a meeting, I want to find the fastest route, so I can arrive on time without stress."
- "When I come home exhausted from work, I want to eat something warm and nutritious, so I can feel like a functioning adult."
- "When I'm managing a team of 20, I want to see who's working on what, so I can allocate resources effectively."

### Functional, Emotional, and Social Dimensions

Every job has multiple dimensions:

- **Functional jobs**: Practical tasks or specific outcomes (e.g., "get from point A to point B")
- **Emotional jobs**: How the person wants to feel or avoid feeling (e.g., "feel confident," "avoid embarrassment")
- **Social jobs**: How the person wants to be perceived by others (e.g., "look successful," "be seen as tech-savvy")

Products typically serve all three dimensions simultaneously. A Tesla is hired for functional transportation, emotional feelings of innovation and environmental responsibility, and social signaling about wealth and values. Ignoring any dimension can lead to products that work functionally but fail emotionally.

### Hiring and Firing Criteria

Customers evaluate products against criteria that matter to them. When the criteria change or a new alternative appears, they "fire" the current solution:

- **Hiring criteria**: What makes a customer choose this product over alternatives?
- **Firing criteria**: What causes a customer to switch to a competitor or abandon the category entirely?

### The Process Theory of Jobs

 JTBD also encompasses the idea that the "job" has a temporal process dimension:

1. **The situation**: Life as it is today
2. **The struggle**: The dissatisfaction with the current approach
3. **The search**: Looking for alternative solutions
4. **The evaluation**: Comparing options
5. **The purchase**: Deciding on a solution
6. **The consumption**: Using the product
7. **The resolution**: The new equilibrium after the job is done (or not)

Understanding this process helps identify intervention points—moments where the product can be present at the moment of struggle or search, not just the moment of purchase.

## How It Works

### Customer Discovery for JTBD

JTBD requires deep customer research, not just surveys or feature requests:

- **Outcome-driven interviews**: Ask customers about their progress, not your product. "Walk me through the last time you [attempted to do the job]."
- **Avoid solutioning in the question**: Don't ask "Would you use a feature that X?" Instead ask "What makes that difficult today?"
- **Observe behavior**: What people do is more reliable than what they say they do
- **Focus on failure**: Understanding why people switch or abandon products reveals what truly matters

### Positioning Around Jobs

Products positioned around jobs are differentiated in meaningful ways:

```
Traditional positioning: "We're a project management tool for software teams"

Job-based positioning: "When engineers and managers have to coordinate 
across time zones without a meeting, we help them align on priorities 
so the right work gets done without endless status updates."
```

## Practical Applications

- **Product strategy**: Identifying unmet jobs that represent innovation opportunities
- **Feature prioritization**: Evaluating features by how well they serve specific jobs
- **Messaging and marketing**: Communicating in terms of the job, not the product category
- **Competitive analysis**: Understanding which jobs competitors serve well or poorly
- **Segmentation**: Segmenting customers by the job they need done, not demographics

## Examples

```markdown
# Example: JTBD analysis for a food delivery app

## Job Statement
"When I don't have time or energy to cook but want a proper meal, 
I want to order food from restaurants I trust, so I can eat well 
without the effort of going out."

## Hiring Criteria (what keeps customers)
- Wide selection of restaurants
- Reliable delivery time (±10 minutes)
- Food arrives hot and fresh
- Easy reordering of favorites
- Transparent pricing with no surprise fees

## Firing Criteria (what causes churn)
- Wrong order delivered
- Food arrives cold
- Restaurant quality inconsistent
- Delivery takes more than 60 minutes
- App crashes during checkout

## Emotional Dimension
- "I want to feel like I'm treating myself, not just surviving"
- "I want to impress guests without cooking"

## Social Dimension  
- "I want my friends to see I have good taste in food"
- "I don't want to seem lazy for ordering in"

## Innovation Opportunities
- AI recommendation engine based on mood and occasion
- "Restaurant quality score" based on delivery packaging and arrival temp
- Subscription for "healthy meal of the week" automation
```

## Personal Notes

The most transformative thing about JTBD is that it shifts the question from "what features should we build?" to "what progress are our customers trying to make that we're not fully enabling?" It's humbling because it often reveals that your biggest competitor isn't another product—it's "doing nothing" or "using a workaround." I've found it most powerful when used not just for product decisions but for marketing and sales: understanding the job changes how you talk about a product entirely, and the job-based positioning often resonates far more deeply than feature-based messaging.
