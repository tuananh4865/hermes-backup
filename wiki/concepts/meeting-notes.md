# Meeting Notes

## Overview

Meeting notes capture the key points, decisions, and action items from meetings, serving as a record that enables follow-up, accountability, and institutional memory. Effective meeting notes transform conversations into actionable documentation, ensuring that insights and agreements made in real-time are not lost and can be referenced later by participants and stakeholders who could not attend.

Meeting notes are a form of lightweight documentation that balances the need for accurate records against the cost of capturing every detail. The goal is not a transcript but a curated summary that captures what matters: what was decided, what needs to happen next, and any blockers or concerns raised.

## Key Concepts

**Meeting Context** establishes the foundation for useful notes. This includes the meeting title, date, duration, attendees, and the meeting's purpose or agenda. Without context, future readers cannot assess the relevance or applicability of the decisions made.

**Decision Records** capture what was agreed upon, including the rationale when relevant. Decisions should be recorded with enough detail that someone reading the notes a month later understands what was decided and why. Using explicit language like "DECIDED:" or "AGREED:" makes decisions easy to scan.

**Action Items** track specific tasks arising from the meeting, including the owner (person responsible) and deadline. Action items transform discussion into work. Without assigned owners and due dates, action items tend to slip through the cracks.

**Parking Lot** is a section for topics that arise but cannot be adequately addressed during the meeting time. Parking lot items ensure ideas are preserved for future discussion without derailing the current meeting's focus.

**Attendance and Absence** matters because decisions may need to be validated by people who were not present, and stakeholders may need to be informed after the fact. Recording who attended and who was invited but could not attend enables appropriate follow-up.

## How It Works

Taking effective meeting notes requires preparation and a clear template. Before the meeting, review the agenda and prepare a structure that aligns with discussion topics. During the meeting, focus on capturing decisions and action items rather than trying to transcribe everything. Use abbreviations and shorthand, filling in details immediately after the meeting while memory is fresh.

The note-taking process should not interfere with participation. If taking notes prevents you from contributing to the discussion, consider asking someone else to take notes or using audio recording (with permission) as a backup. After the meeting, clean up notes, correct any misunderstandings, and distribute to relevant parties promptly—within 24 hours is ideal.

Template structure for meeting notes:

```markdown
# Meeting Notes: [Topic]
**Date:** YYYY-MM-DD
**Time:** HH:MM - HH:MM
**Attendees:** Name1, Name2, Name3
**Facilitator:** Name

## Agenda
1. Topic A
2. Topic B

## Discussion Summary
### Topic A
- Key point discussed
- Decision made: ...

### Topic B
- Concern raised: ...

## Decisions
- [ ] DECIDED: Decision text (Owner)

## Action Items
- [ ] ACTION: Task description @Owner by DueDate

## Parking Lot
- Topic to discuss later

## Next Meeting
- Date/Time if scheduled
```

## Practical Applications

Meeting notes serve multiple audiences and purposes. Participants use notes to remember what they agreed to do and to track others' commitments. Absent stakeholders rely on notes for context and to stay informed. Managers use notes to track team progress and identify blockers. Organizations use notes as institutional memory that preserves decision rationale for future reference.

In distributed teams across time zones, meeting notes become even more critical because synchronous discussion is expensive. Notes enable asynchronous follow-up and ensure work continues between meetings.

## Examples

A well-structured meeting note captures decisions precisely:

```
## Decisions
- DECIDED: Migrate user authentication to OAuth 2.0 by Q3
- DECIDED: Use PostgreSQL as primary database for new services
- DECIDED: Feature flag rollout for payment redesign starting next sprint

## Action Items
- [ ] @Alice: Draft OAuth migration plan by April 20
- [ ] @Bob: Set up PostgreSQL staging environment by April 22
- [ ] @Carol: Create feature flag configuration by April 25
```

This format makes it immediately clear what was agreed and who is responsible for what, enabling quick scanning and easy tracking.

## Personal Notes

The biggest mistake with meeting notes is treating them as a formality rather than a tool. Notes that sit in documents no one reads are worthless. To make notes valuable: share them promptly, reference them in subsequent meetings to show continuity, and follow up on action items explicitly. Consider using collaborative tools that notify relevant people when notes are published. The discipline of good meeting notes reflects and reinforces a culture of accountability.
