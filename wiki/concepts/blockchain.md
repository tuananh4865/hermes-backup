## Overview

A blockchain is a distributed ledger technology that records transactions across multiple computers in a way that makes them resistant to modification or tampering. Each block in the chain contains a cryptographic hash of the previous block, transaction data, and a timestamp, creating an immutable and chronological record. This structure ensures transparency and security without requiring a central authority, making blockchains particularly valuable for scenarios where trust, traceability, and decentralization are paramount.

The concept was popularized by the anonymous person or group known as Satoshi Nakamoto, who introduced [[Bitcoin]] in 2008 as the first peer-to-peer digital currency built on blockchain technology. Since then, the technology has evolved far beyond cryptocurrency, finding applications in supply chain management, healthcare, voting systems, identity verification, and [[smart contracts]]. Blockchains represent a fundamental shift in how information can be stored and verified, moving from centralized databases to decentralized, consensus-driven architectures.

## How It Works

A blockchain operates through a combination of cryptographic hashing, distributed networking, and consensus mechanisms. When a transaction is initiated, it is broadcast to a network of computers known as nodes. These nodes validate the transaction using established consensus rules, ensuring that the sender has sufficient funds and that the transaction follows protocol specifications.

Once validated, transactions are combined into a new block along with other pending transactions. This block is assigned a cryptographic hash—a unique digital fingerprint generated by a [[hash function]]—which incorporates the hash of the previous block, creating a chain linkage. This hashing process ensures that altering any historical block would invalidate all subsequent hashes, making the ledger tamper-evident.

The consensus mechanism is the protocol by which nodes agree on the current state of the ledger. [[Proof of Work]] requires participants (miners) to solve complex mathematical puzzles to validate blocks, consuming significant computational energy. [[Proof of Stake]] selects validators based on the amount of cryptocurrency they hold and are willing to "stake" as collateral, offering a more energy-efficient alternative. Other consensus models include Delegated Proof of Stake, Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance, and proof-of-authority systems, each with different trade-offs in decentralization, speed, and security.

After consensus is reached, the block is added to the existing chain and distributed to all participating nodes. This replicated ledger ensures that no single point of failure can compromise the integrity of the system.

## Types

Blockchains can be broadly categorized based on their access permissions and network architecture.

**Public blockchains** are open networks that anyone can join, participate in, and view. They operate in a fully decentralized manner with no central entity controlling the network. Bitcoin and [[Ethereum]] are the most prominent public blockchains, enabling permissionless innovation where developers can build applications without requiring approval. These networks prioritize censorship resistance, transparency, and decentralization, though they often face scalability challenges due to their distributed nature.

**Private blockchains** restrict network participation, with a single entity or consortium controlling who can read, write, and validate transactions. These are often adopted by enterprises seeking the benefits of blockchain technology—such as immutability and traceability—while maintaining regulatory compliance or operational confidentiality. Private blockchains offer higher transaction throughput and lower latency but require trust in the controlling entity. Examples include Hyperledger Fabric and R3 Corda, commonly used in financial services and supply chain applications.

**Consortium blockchains** represent a hybrid model where multiple organizations jointly govern the network. Decision-making is distributed among a pre-selected group of validators, balancing decentralization with accountability. This makes consortium chains suitable for industry collaborations, such as banking consortia or cross-organization supply chain tracking systems.
