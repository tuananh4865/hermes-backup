# Porter's Five Forces

## Overview

Porter's Five Forces is a strategic framework developed by Harvard Business School professor Michael E. Porter in 1979 to analyze an industry's competitive structure and identify where power lies between buyers, suppliers, substitutes, and new entrants. Rather than evaluating a single company, this framework examines the underlying profitability drivers of an entire market—helping executives decide whether to enter, stay in, or exit an industry.

Porter introduced this model in his book "Competitive Strategy" as an evolution beyond simple SWOT analysis, which treats all factors as roughly equal. Five Forces provides a more rigorous lens: it systematically evaluates five structural dimensions that determine competitive intensity and profitability. The framework remains a cornerstone of business strategy education and is routinely used in investment analysis, corporate planning, and market entry decisions.

## Key Concepts

### The Five Forces

**1. Competitive Rivalry** measures the intensity of competition among existing players. High rivalry (like the airline or smartphone industries) erodes margins and demands constant differentiation. Low rivalry (like utility companies in regulated markets) allows stable returns.

**2. Supplier Power** assesses how much leverage suppliers hold. Concentrated supplier markets (like semiconductor fabrication), switching costs, or unique capabilities give suppliers pricing power. Fragmented supplier markets with commoditized inputs keep power with buyers.

**3. Buyer Power** evaluates customer ability to force prices down or demand higher quality. Large, sophisticated buyers with easy switching options (like enterprise software purchasing committees) can commoditize vendors against each other.

**4. Threat of Substitution** examines alternative products or services that solve the same problem. Close substitutes limit pricing power; distant substitutes (e.g., video calls substituting for business travel) can disrupt entire industries.

**5. Threat of New Entrants** gauges how easily new competitors can enter the market. High barriers (patents, regulations, capital requirements, network effects) protect incumbents; low barriers invite constant competitive pressure.

### Industry Profitability

Porter's core insight: **industry structure determines profitability, not just product quality or operational efficiency**. A company in a structurally attractive industry will outperform an excellent company in a terrible industry. This is why competitors with similar strategies and resources can have vastly different financial results depending on their industry positioning.

## How It Works

Using Five Forces analysis involves systematically rating each force on a Low/Medium/High scale and identifying the dominant forces in your industry.

## Practical Applications

- **Market Entry Decisions**: Before acquiring a company or launching in a new market, Five Forces reveals whether the target industry offers sustainable returns.
- **Strategic Positioning**: Identifying which forces are most powerful helps focus strategy. If supplier power is the issue, vertical integration or supplier diversification becomes priority.
- **Investment Analysis**: Investors use Five Forces to assess moats and sustainability of returns before investing.
- **Competitive Intelligence**: Understanding rivals' positions relative to the five forces guides resource allocation.

## Examples

**Highly Attractive Industry Example**: Enterprise software SaaS with high switching costs, network effects creating winner-take-all dynamics, and regulatory barriers protecting incumbents. Microsoft, Salesforce, and SAP operate in relatively attractive industry structures.

**Highly Unattractive Industry Example**: Commercial airlines face intense rivalry, powerful unions (suppliers), price-sensitive travelers (buyers), and video conferencing as a substitute (especially post-COVID). Margins are thin and constant.

**Technology Disruption Example**: The music industry illustrates how substitution (streaming vs. physical albums) completely reshuffled industry profitability and power dynamics.

## Personal Notes

The biggest mistake people make with Five Forces is treating it as a static snapshot. Industries evolve—regulatory changes, technological disruption, and globalization constantly shift the balance of power. I like to run Three Forces analysis annually on my core markets and build scenarios for how the forces might shift in 3-5 years. Also worth noting: Porter has since updated his views on shared value, arguing that companies can't just exploit industry structure—they need to build ecosystems that benefit all stakeholders.
