## Overview

A resume is a concise document that summarizes your professional history, skills, and qualifications for potential employers. It serves as your primary marketing tool during a job search, presenting your value proposition in a structured format. Unlike a curriculum vitae (CV), which is more comprehensive and used primarily in academic or medical fields, a resume is typically limited to one or two pages and tailored to specific job applications. The primary purpose of a resume is to secure an interview by demonstrating that your experience and abilities align with the position requirements.

Resumes have become essential in modern hiring processes, where recruiters often spend less than ten seconds scanning each document. A well-crafted resume communicates your professional brand and makes a strong first impression, while a poorly constructed one can disqualify you from consideration even before the interview stage.

## Key Sections

A standard resume includes several essential components that hiring managers expect to find:

**Contact Information**: Your full name, phone number, email address, and location (city and state). LinkedIn profile and professional website are increasingly common additions.

**Professional Summary or Objective**: A brief statement of your career goals and key qualifications, typically three to four sentences.

**Work Experience**: Reverse chronological listing of your positions, including company name, job title, dates of employment, and bullet points describing responsibilities and achievements.

**Education**: Your degrees, institutions attended, graduation dates, and relevant academic achievements or certifications.

**Skills**: Technical and soft skills relevant to the target position, often presented as keyword-rich bullet points or a skills matrix.

Optional sections include certifications, professional affiliations, projects, volunteer work, and technical proficiencies. The specific sections you include should align with the requirements of your target industry and the specific job posting.

## Best Practices

**Quantify Your Achievements**: Use concrete numbers, percentages, and metrics to demonstrate impact. Instead of "improved sales," write "increased quarterly sales by 23%." Quantified achievements provide evidence of your capabilities and make your contributions tangible to recruiters.

**Customize for Each Application**: Tailor your resume to each position by incorporating keywords from the job description. This alignment signals to both ATS systems and human readers that you are a strong match for the role.

**Use Action Verbs**: Begin bullet points with strong action verbs such as "led," "developed," "implemented," or "optimized" to convey initiative and competence.

**Maintain Clean Formatting**: Use consistent fonts, adequate white space, and clear section headings. Avoid graphics, tables, or complex formatting that may break during ATS parsing.

**Keep It Concise**: Limit your resume to one page for early-career professionals and two pages for those with extensive experience. Every line should add value by showcasing relevant qualifications.

## ATS

Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) are software platforms that employers use to manage and filter job applications. Most large organizations and many smaller companies process resumes through ATS before a human recruiter ever sees them. Understanding ATS optimization is critical for job seekers in today's market.

To make your resume ATS-friendly, use standard section headings such as "Work Experience" and "Education" rather than creative alternatives. Submit your resume in formats compatible with ATS parsing, typically plain text (.txt) or Microsoft Word (.docx). Incorporate keywords naturally throughout your document, drawing from the job description and industry-standard terminology. Avoid headers and footers, as these elements often get stripped during parsing. Test your resume by converting it to plain text to verify that all content remains intact and properly formatted.
