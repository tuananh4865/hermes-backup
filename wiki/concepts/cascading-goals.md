# Cascading Goals

## Overview

Cascading goals is the organizational practice of aligning objectives from the top of an organization down through each layer — from company-level strategy to department teams to individual contributors — ensuring that every person's work meaningfully contributes to overarching organizational priorities. The term comes from the metaphor of water cascading down a series of connected pools: the goals flow downward, connecting and linking each level while maintaining a clear line of sight to the highest-level objectives.

The practice is central to [[OKRs]] (Objectives and Key Results) and similar goal-setting frameworks like Balanced Scorecards. Without a deliberate cascading mechanism, organizations frequently suffer from alignment fragmentation — teams optimize for locally visible metrics while organizational priorities suffer. Cascading goals makes strategy tangible at every level by forcing explicit articulation of how each team's work connects to broader ambitions.

## Key Concepts

**Line of Sight:** The most important quality of effective cascading goals. An individual contributor should be able to trace a clear, logical path from their daily work to the company's top-level objectives. Without this line of sight, motivation suffers and effort becomes fragmented.

**Alignment vs. Consistency:** Cascading doesn't mean every goal looks the same at every level. Company objectives might be qualitative and aspirational ("become the most trusted cloud platform for enterprises"), while team-level key results are quantitative and measurable ("reduce p99 API latency to under 200ms"). The key is alignment — shared direction — not identical goals.

**Top-Down and Bottom-Up:** While cascading flows primarily top-down, the best implementations also encourage bottom-up input. Teams should have input into goals that affect them, and individual contributors should help decompose higher-level objectives into actionable tasks. Pure top-down mandated goals tend to suffer from poor buy-in and contextual blind spots.

**Horizontal Dependencies:** Cascading goals must also account for cross-functional dependencies. If the sales team's goal depends on marketing delivering leads, and engineering's goal depends on sales providing customer feedback, the cascading process must make these horizontal linkages explicit.

**Differentiated Velocity:** Different levels of the organization may set goals on different cycles. Company-level OKRs might be annual, team-level might be quarterly, and individual-level might be monthly. The cadence should match the speed at which that level can meaningfully evaluate progress.

## How It Works

The cascading process typically follows this structure:

1. **Company-level objectives are defined** by leadership, ideally tied directly to the organization's mission and annual strategy. These are aspirational and motivating.
2. **Department/team objectives** are derived from company objectives. Each team identifies 2-5 objectives they uniquely own or significantly contribute to.
3. **Individual contributor goals** are set in consultation with managers, directly supporting team objectives.
4. **Key results** are defined at each level — specific, measurable, time-bound metrics that indicate success.
5. **Check-ins and reviews** happen throughout the cycle to track progress, identify blockers, and course-correct.

```markdown
# Example Cascading Goal Structure

## Company Objective (Annual)
"Scale from $10M to $50M ARR while maintaining customer satisfaction"

## Engineering Team Objective (Quarterly)
"Build the infrastructure platform to support 5x traffic growth"

### Key Results:
- KR1: Migrate 100% of services to Kubernetes (from 40%)
- KR2: Achieve 99.95% uptime across all production services
- KR3: Reduce infrastructure cost per user by 40%

## Individual IC Goal (Monthly)
"Complete database sharding migration for orders service"

### Contributing to: KR1
### Owner: Senior DB Engineer
### Status: On track
```

## Practical Applications

- **Annual planning:** Organizations use cascading goals during annual planning to translate multi-year strategy into year-one initiatives
- **OKR cycles:** [[OKRs]] rely on cascading as their core mechanism — quarterly cycles with explicit alignment checking
- **Reorgs and mergers:** After organizational changes, cascading goals help re-align teams to new structures and priorities
- **Startup scaling:** As companies grow past 50-100 people, informal goal-setting breaks down and explicit cascading becomes necessary
- **Strategy execution:** Turning a CEO's strategic vision into executable engineering roadmap items requires explicit cascading

## Examples

- **Google:** Famous for popularizing OKRs; each quarter objectives cascade from Larry Page's top-level down through the entire company, with public scoring making progress visible
- **Intel:** Andy Grove's "MBO" (Management by Objectives) system at Intel influenced many modern cascading goal systems; Grove emphasized "constructional resistance" (pushing back on infeasible top-down goals)
- **Spotify:** Squad model with explicit "chapter" and "tribe" alignment to avoid siloed optimization
- **Amazon:** Two-pizza teams with well-defined team interfaces; goals cascade through annual planning and PR/FAQ process

## Personal Notes

The hardest part of cascading goals isn't writing them — it's maintaining genuine alignment when priorities shift mid-cycle. I've seen teams complete a full cascading exercise, only to have leadership shift strategy six weeks later, rendering the entire goal tree stale. The answer isn't to avoid cascading — it's to treat goals as living documents with built-in review cadences. Also worth noting: cascading goals is a forcing function for honest conversations about priorities. If you can't articulate how a team's work connects to company objectives, that's often a signal of deeper organizational misalignment worth investigating.
