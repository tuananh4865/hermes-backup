# OKR (Objectives and Key Results)

## Overview

OKR (Objectives and Key Results) is a goal-setting framework that helps organizations, teams, and individuals define and track objectives alongside measurable outcomes. Originally developed at Intel by Andy Grove in the 1970s and popularized by John Doerr who brought the framework to Google in 1999, OKRs have become one of the most widely adopted management methodologies in the technology industry and beyond. The framework provides a simple but powerful structure for setting ambitious goals, creating alignment throughout an organization, and measuring progress in a way that encourages focus without stifling innovation.

An Objective is a qualitative, inspirational statement that describes what you want to achieve. It should be memorable, motivational, and capable of inspiring effort. A Key Result is a quantitative metric that defines how you will know if the objective has been achieved. Together, they create a pairing where the Objective provides direction and meaning while the Key Results provide accountability and measurement.

OKRs are typically set quarterly, creating a regular cadence for reflection, adjustment, and ambition. At the end of each period, teams and individuals score their Key Results, typically on a 0-1 scale, and use this feedback to inform the next cycle's goals. This continuous cycle of setting, executing, and reviewing drives organizational learning and keeps teams focused on what matters most.

## Key Concepts

**Objectives** are the "what" of the framework—they describe the direction you want to go. Good objectives are:
- Qualitative and inspiring, not numerical
- Ambitious and slightly uncomfortable to achieve
- Actionable by the team or individual who owns them
- Time-bound (typically quarterly)

An objective might be "Become the industry leader in customer satisfaction" or "Transform our engineering organization for scale." These statements provide meaning and context for the work that follows.

**Key Results** are the "how" and "how much"—specific metrics that prove whether the objective has been achieved. Good Key Results are:
- Specific and measurable (not vague aspirations)
- Outcome-oriented, not just activities
- Difficult to achieve but not impossible (70% achievement should feel ambitious)
- Limited in number (typically 2-5 per objective)

A Key Result might be "Achieve Net Promoter Score of 50 or higher" or "Reduce p99 API latency to under 200ms." These provide the concrete evidence of success.

**Cascading OKRs** refers to the practice of aligning objectives across organizational levels. Company-level objectives cascade to department-level, which cascade to team-level, which cascade to individual-level. This creates alignment where individual work connects to team goals, which connect to department goals, which connect to company strategy. While cascading, objectives can remain the same or be differentiated, but the Key Results should show how each level contributes to the higher-level goal.

**OKR Scoring** typically uses a 0-1 scale where 0 means nothing was achieved and 1 means everything was achieved. The intended aspiration is usually 0.6-0.7—"Comfortable" achievement. Scoring significantly below this suggests the objective was too ambitious or execution failed; scoring significantly above suggests the objective wasn't ambitious enough.

## How It Works

A typical quarterly OKR cycle follows this pattern:

1. **Setting**: At the start of the quarter, teams and individuals draft their objectives based on company strategy and team priorities. These are discussed, refined, and finalized through dialogue between managers and reports. The goal is ambitious but achievable alignment.

2. **Execution**: Throughout the quarter, teams work toward their Key Results. Progress is checked regularly (weekly is common), not to enforce accountability but to identify obstacles and provide support. Key Results are not changed mid-quarter unless circumstances fundamentally shift.

3. **Review**: At the end of the quarter, teams score their Key Results and reflect on what was accomplished. This review examines what worked, what didn't, and what was learned. The review feeds into the next cycle's planning.

```markdown
# Example: Engineering Team OKRs

## Objective 1: Dramatically improve platform reliability
| Key Result | Baseline | Target | Q1 Score |
|------------|----------|--------|----------|
| Reduce customer-affecting incidents | 12/month | 4/month | 0.7 |
| Achieve 99.95% API availability | 99.7% | 99.95% | 0.6 |

| Key Result | Baseline | Target | Q1 Score |

| Increase deployment frequency | 2/week | Daily | 0.5 |
| Reduce lead time for changes | 2 weeks | 3 days | 0.4 |

```

## Practical Applications

**Company Strategy Execution** is the most common use of OKRs. Leadership sets company-level objectives that define the strategic direction for the year, then departments and teams create supporting OKRs. This ensures that daily work connects to strategic priorities and that resources are allocated to what matters.

**Product Development** teams use OKRs to balance feature development with platform improvements and technical debt. A product team might have one objective focused on customer outcomes (new acquisition metrics) and another on platform quality (performance improvements). OKRs force explicit prioritization between these competing demands.

**Personal Productivity** applications of OKRs help individuals focus their efforts. Whether for personal development, career growth, or side projects, individuals can apply the OKR framework to maintain clarity on what matters most and measure their progress.

**Cross-Functional Initiatives** benefit from OKRs because they create a shared language and measurable goals across teams with different priorities. When marketing, engineering, and sales all have OKRs that reference shared metrics (like "leads converted to customers" or "customer satisfaction"), collaboration improves.

## Examples

A SaaS startup might set these company-level OKRs for a quarter:

**Objective: Establish product-market fit in the enterprise segment**

- Key Result 1: Achieve 5 enterprise customers with contracts over $100K ARR (currently 0)
- Key Result 2: Reach 80% enterprise feature completion on roadmap (currently at 45%)
- Key Result 3: Net Promoter Score of 40+ from enterprise customers (currently measuring baseline)

An engineering team supporting this objective might set:

**Objective: Build the foundation for enterprise-grade platform**

- Key Result 1: Complete SOC 2 Type II compliance audit (0% → 100%)
- Key Result 2: Implement SSO and SAML authentication (in progress → complete)
- Key Result 3: Achieve 99.9% uptime for core API (currently 99.5%)

## Personal Notes

OKRs fail when they become a bureaucratic exercise divorced from actual work. The biggest mistake is treating Key Results as a todo list of projects rather than measurable outcomes. Also, don't stack too many objectives—three per quarter per team is usually the limit before focus dissolves. Finally, score honestly; inflated scores undermine the learning feedback loop.
