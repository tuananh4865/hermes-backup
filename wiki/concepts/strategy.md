# strategy

## Overview

Strategy is a fundamental concept that describes the set of choices an organization makes to achieve its objectives and gain competitive advantage. In both business and technology contexts, strategy provides a guiding framework for allocating resources, prioritizing initiatives, and making decisions under uncertainty. It bridges the gap between an organization's current state and its desired future state, serving as a roadmap for sustained success.

At its core, strategy involves understanding where you are today, where you want to go, and how you will get there. This requires analyzing internal capabilities and external conditions, defining clear goals, and selecting among alternative courses of action. Strategy is not merely about planning—it is about making deliberate choices that define what you will do and, just as importantly, what you will not do. In competitive environments, effective strategy creates differentiation and value that is difficult for rivals to replicate.

Strategy operates across multiple levels within an organization. Corporate strategy addresses the overall scope and direction of the enterprise, deciding which industries to compete in and how businesses should be combined. Business unit strategy focuses on how to compete effectively within a specific market or product category. Functional strategy guides the actions of individual departments such as marketing, technology, or operations. Each level must align with the others to ensure organizational coherence and maximum impact.

## Frameworks

Strategic frameworks provide structured approaches for analyzing situations and developing action plans. They help organizations move beyond intuition and systematically evaluate complex environments.

**SWOT Analysis** is one of the most widely used strategic planning tools. It examines an organization's Strengths and Weaknesses—internal factors that are within the organization's control—alongside Opportunities and Threats, which are external conditions that the organization must respond to. By mapping these four dimensions, organizations can identify areas where they have competitive advantage, areas requiring improvement, market trends to exploit, and challenges to mitigate. The power of SWOT lies in its simplicity, though it works best when used to inform rather than replace deeper strategic thinking.

**Porter's Five Forces** provides a framework for analyzing industry competition and profitability. Developed by Michael Porter, this model evaluates five key forces: the threat of new entrants, the bargaining power of buyers, the bargaining power of suppliers, the threat of substitute products or services, and the intensity of competitive rivalry. Understanding these forces helps organizations position themselves strategically within an industry, identifying whether the environment favors growth and profits or presents structural challenges. Strong performance against these forces often correlates with sustainable competitive advantage.

**OKR (Objectives and Key Results)** is a goal-setting framework originally developed at Intel and popularized by Google. It consists of two components: an Objective, which defines what you want to achieve and should be inspiring and qualitative, and Key Results, which are specific, measurable metrics that indicate whether the objective has been met. OKRs are typically set quarterly and cascaded throughout the organization, creating alignment from corporate level down to individual contributors. This framework bridges strategic planning and execution, ensuring that high-level goals translate into tangible outcomes.

## Technology Strategy

Technology strategy addresses how an organization uses technology to achieve its business objectives and maintain competitive advantage. It encompasses decisions about technology investments, architecture, capabilities, and sourcing that shape the organization's ability to innovate and operate effectively.

A technology strategy must align closely with overall business strategy. When an organization aims to differentiate through customer experience, technology investments may prioritize user-facing systems and digital channels. When the strategy emphasizes operational efficiency, technology may focus on automation, process optimization, and data analytics. The key is ensuring that technology decisions directly support the organization's strategic priorities rather than pursued independently.

Modern technology strategy increasingly considers digital transformation, cloud computing, data as a strategic asset, and cybersecurity. Organizations must also address technology architecture decisions—whether to build custom solutions, purchase commercial software, or leverage open-source platforms. Technology strategy also encompasses talent and organizational capabilities, recognizing that technology investments require skilled people and adaptive organizational structures to deliver value.
