# Reading

## Overview

Reading is the process of consuming and interpreting written text to extract meaning, acquire knowledge, and build understanding. It is a foundational skill for [[learning]] and serves as the primary mechanism for [[knowledge]] transfer across generations, disciplines, and cultures. Effective reading goes beyond simply decoding words on a page; it involves active engagement with the material, critical evaluation of arguments, and integration of new information with existing knowledge structures.

In the context of personal development and education, reading encompasses a spectrum from casual information browsing to rigorous academic study. The value one derives from reading depends not only on the quality of the source material but also on the reader's approach and technique. A skilled reader adapts their reading strategy to their goals, whether that involves quickly surveying a document for relevance, deeply analyzing a complex argument, or extracting actionable information from a technical text.

## Techniques

Different reading techniques serve different purposes, and effective readers develop a repertoire of strategies they can deploy based on their objectives.

**Skimming** is a rapid reading technique used to get a general overview of material. The reader moves quickly through the text, focusing on headings, topic sentences, and key phrases while skipping supporting details. Skimming is useful for deciding whether a source is worth deeper examination or for refreshing memory on material already studied. It should not be confused with careless rushing; even when skimming, attention to structure and signal words helps capture the main ideas.

**Deep Reading** involves slow, careful, and focused engagement with a text. This technique is essential for complex material that requires full comprehension, such as philosophical arguments, scientific papers, or literary analysis. Deep reading demands sustained attention and allows the reader to follow intricate lines of reasoning, appreciate nuance, and absorb detailed information. It is a deliberate practice that builds comprehension over time.

**Active Reading** transforms reading from a passive activity into an interactive process. Active readers engage with the text by asking questions, challenging assumptions, making predictions, and connecting ideas to their own experience. This technique improves retention and understanding by requiring the brain to process information at deeper levels. Strategies include annotating text, visualizing concepts, and pausing to summarize sections in one's own words.

## Note-Taking

Note-taking complements reading by creating external records of insights, summaries, and questions that arise during the reading process. Effective note-taking systems help organize information for later retrieval and reinforce learning through the act of summarizing. Popular methods include the [[Cornell Method]], which divides notes into cues, notes, and summary sections, and the [[Zettelkasten]] system, which emphasizes linking ideas across multiple notes to build a connected knowledge base. The choice of method matters less than the discipline of recording notes consistently and reviewing them regularly.
