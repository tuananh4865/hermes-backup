# OKRs

## Overview

OKRs, short for Objectives and Key Results, are a goal-setting framework designed to align individual, team, and organizational efforts toward ambitious, measurable outcomes. Originally pioneered by Andy Grove at Intel and popularized by John Doerr at Google, OKRs have become one of the most widely adopted management methodologies for driving focus, accountability, and results in organizations of all sizes.

The framework operates on a simple but powerful premise: define what you want to achieve (the Objective) and specify how you will measure success (the Key Results). Unlike traditional goal-setting approaches that rely on subjective performance reviews or vague aspirations, OKRs demand measurable evidence of progress. This makes them particularly valuable in fast-moving environments where teams need clear direction without losing flexibility in how they achieve their targets.

OKRs are typically set on a quarterly cycle, allowing organizations to maintain strategic focus while remaining adaptable to changing circumstances. At the end of each cycle, teams and individuals score their Key Results to evaluate performance and inform the next round of goal-setting. This cadence creates a continuous feedback loop that keeps organizations aligned and responsive.

## Components

OKRs consist of two interrelated components that work together to drive focus and accountability.

**Objectives** are inspirational, qualitative statements that describe what you want to accomplish. A good Objective is ambitious, motivating, and aligned with the broader mission of the organization. Objectives should be short enough to remember and compelling enough to rally a team around a common purpose. They answer the question: "Where do we want to go?"

**Key Results** are quantitative metrics that define how you will measure success. Each Objective typically has two to five Key Results, and these must be specific, measurable, and time-bound. Key Results should be outcome-focused rather than task-focused, meaning they measure the result or impact of actions rather than the activities themselves. They answer the question: "How will we know we got there?"

Together, an Objective and its associated Key Results form a unit of goal-setting. The Objective provides direction and inspiration, while the Key Results provide a concrete, measurable definition of success.

## Setting OKRs

Effective OKRs require careful crafting to avoid common pitfalls. Follow these principles to set high-quality goals.

Start with alignment. Objectives should cascade from the organizational level down through teams and individuals, ensuring that every goal contributes to broader strategic priorities. Use [[Cascading Goals]] as a framework to ensure vertical alignment across levels.

Make Objectives ambitious but achievable. The most effective OKRs push organizations toward meaningful stretch goals without becoming completely unrealistic. A common guideline is that achieving 60-70% of Key Results should represent strong performance, indicating that the goals were genuinely challenging.

Key Results must be measurable and verifiable. Use specific numbers, percentages, or ratios rather than vague descriptors. Instead of "improve customer satisfaction," a stronger Key Result would be "increase Net Promoter Score from 45 to 55." The use of [[SMART Goals]] criteria can help ensure each Key Result is Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.

Focus on outcomes over outputs. Key Results should measure the impact of your work, not the activities you performed. A team that completes many tasks but delivers little measurable impact has not achieved their Key Results, regardless of how busy they were.

Finally, involve the people who will be accountable for achieving the OKRs in the setting process. Collaborative goal-setting increases commitment and ensures that those closest to the work have input into what success looks like.

## OKR vs KPI

While OKRs and [[KPI]]s (Key Performance Indicators) both involve measurable goals, they serve different purposes and operate on different timescales.

**Purpose and Orientation**: OKRs are goal-setting tools designed to drive change and achieve specific outcomes within a defined period, typically a quarter. They are aspirational and focus on what needs to be accomplished to move the organization forward. KPIs, on the other hand, are ongoing performance metrics that track whether business-as-usual operations are on track. They measure the health of existing processes and are monitored continuously rather than set and reviewed cyclically.

**Ambition Level**: OKRs typically involve stretch goals that push beyond current performance levels. They ask the question: "What should we achieve if we really raise the bar?" KPIs tend to represent baseline expectations or acceptable performance ranges. Falling below a KPI threshold signals a problem, while exceeding a KPI is not necessarily the goal.

**Number and Scope**: Organizations typically maintain a small number of active OKRs at any given time, usually three to five per level, to maintain focus. KPIs can be numerous and are often embedded in dashboards that track many aspects of business performance simultaneously.

**Review Cadence**: OKRs are reviewed at the end of each cycle (quarterly) with a formal scoring process. KPIs are reviewed more frequently, often weekly or monthly, as part of operational management.

The two frameworks complement each other well. KPIs can form the basis for Key Results within OKRs, providing measurable indicators that feed into the goal-setting process. However, treating them as interchangeable undermines the distinct value each provides.
