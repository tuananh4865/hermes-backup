# Planning

Planning is the systematic process of defining goals, identifying resources, outlining actions, and establishing timelines to achieve specific outcomes. In product and project management contexts, planning serves as the foundation for aligning teams, managing expectations, and driving focused execution. Effective planning transforms abstract ambitions into concrete, achievable work.

## Overview

Planning bridges the gap between vision and execution. It involves assessing current conditions, defining what success looks like, and mapping the path to get there. In product management, planning typically operates across multiple horizons: strategic planning sets long-term direction over quarters or years, while tactical planning focuses on near-term deliverables and sprint-level commitments.

A well-crafted plan answers key questions: What are we building and why? Who is it for? What constraints exist? How will we measure progress? What risks might emerge? Without clear answers, teams struggle with misaligned priorities, wasted effort, and missed deadlines.

Planning also facilitates communication across stakeholders. A visible plan becomes a shared reference point for executives, development teams, designers, and customers alike. It sets expectations and provides a baseline for evaluating whether a project is on track.

## Frameworks

Several established frameworks guide planning efforts:

**OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)** — This goal-setting framework pairs ambitious objectives with measurable key results. Objectives define what you want to achieve; key results quantify how you'll know you succeeded. OKRs work well for aligning entire organizations around bold, time-bound goals. See [[OKRs]] for more.

**RICE Scoring** — RICE prioritizes initiatives by calculating a score based on Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort. The formula (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort yields a numerical ranking that helps teams compare competing ideas objectively. Teams use RICE during [[prioritization]] sessions to decide what lands in a product roadmap. See [[RICE]] for details.

**MoSCoW Method** — This technique categorizes requirements or features into Must-have, Should-have, Could-have, and Won't-have. MoSCoW forces teams to make hard trade-offs and ensures critical deliverables receive focus. It's particularly useful when scope needs to shrink due to time or resource constraints. See [[MoSCoW]].

## Techniques

Common planning techniques include:

- **Roadmapping** — Visual representation of planned initiatives over time. Product roadmaps communicate strategy and intent to both internal teams and external stakeholders. See [[roadmap]].
- **Prioritization matrices** — Tools like the Eisenhower Matrix or value-vs-effort grids help rank tasks by urgency, importance, or return on investment.
- **Decomposition** — Breaking large goals into smaller, manageable deliverables. This reveals hidden complexity and makes estimation more accurate.
- **Timeboxing** — Allocating fixed time periods to work, with scope adjusted to fit the window. Common in agile and Scrum methodologies.
- **Retrospectives** — Reviewing past planning cycles to refine future approaches. Continuous improvement is essential for planning discipline.
