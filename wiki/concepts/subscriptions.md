## Overview

A subscription is a billing arrangement where customers pay recurring fees at set intervals to access a product or service. Unlike one-time purchases, subscriptions create predictable, ongoing revenue streams for businesses. The subscription model became dominant with the rise of [[SaaS]] (Software as a Service), transforming how software is delivered and monetized.

The subscription economy refers to the broader shift from ownership to access-based consumption across industries. This model benefits both parties: businesses gain recurring revenue and better predictability, while customers enjoy continuous updates, support, and often lower upfront costs.

## Models

### Monthly Subscriptions

Customers pay a fixed fee each calendar month. This model offers flexibility and lower perceived commitment, making it attractive for new customers. However, monthly plans typically carry higher effective pricing than longer commitments due to the increased billing frequency and customer acquisition cost amortization.

### Annual Subscriptions

Customers pay for twelve months upfront, usually at a discounted rate compared to monthly billing. Annual plans improve revenue predictability, reduce churn, and simplify financial planning. Many businesses offer tiered annual pricing with increasing discounts for longer commitments.

### Usage-Based Subscriptions

Also known as consumption-based or metered billing, this model charges customers based on actual usage—features accessed, data processed, or API calls made. Usage-based pricing aligns cost with value received and scales with customer needs. It has gained popularity in cloud infrastructure and developer tools, exemplified by platforms like AWS and Twilio.

### Hybrid Models

Many businesses combine multiple approaches, offering base subscription tiers with usage add-ons. For example, a SaaS platform might provide a Standard plan at $49/month with optional overage charges for exceeding storage limits.

## Metrics

### Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)

MRR represents the predictable revenue generated from subscriptions in a given month. It is calculated by summing all active subscription fees, normalized to a monthly figure. MRR growth tracks expansion from new customer acquisition, upgrades, and reactivations.

### Churn Rate

Churn measures the percentage of customers or revenue lost during a period. Customer churn counts cancelled subscriptions, while revenue churn measures the MRR lost. Lower churn indicates higher customer satisfaction and product-market fit. Most healthy SaaS businesses target monthly churn below 5%.

### Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)

LTV estimates the total revenue a business can expect from a customer over the entire relationship. It is calculated by multiplying average revenue per user (ARPU) by average customer lifespan. LTV guides customer acquisition spending and helps evaluate long-term profitability.
