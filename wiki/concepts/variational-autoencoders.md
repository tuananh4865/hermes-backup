# Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

## Overview

Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) are a powerful class of generative models that learn to compress data into compact latent representations while simultaneously learning to generate new samples from that latent space. Unlike traditional autoencoders that learn deterministic encodings, VAEs learn a probability distribution over the latent space, allowing smooth interpolation and controlled generation. Introduced by Kingma and Welling in 2013, VAEs combine variational inference with neural network architectures to enable tractable density estimation and generation.

VAEs provide a principled approach to generative modeling, offering interpretable latent spaces, stable training, and the ability to generate new samples by sampling from learned distributions. They serve as both a standalone generative model and a foundation for more complex architectures in multi-modal learning and representation learning.

## Key Concepts

**Encoder and Decoder Architecture**

The encoder network q_φ(z|x) maps input data x to the parameters of a probability distribution (typically Gaussian) in latent space z. The decoder network p_θ(x|z) reconstructs data from latent samples. Both networks are typically parameterized by deep neural networks, with the encoder outputting mean and log-variance vectors.

**Reparameterization Trick**

Directly sampling from the latent distribution would break gradient flow during backpropagation. The reparameterization trick solves this by expressing the sample as z = μ + σε, where ε ~ N(0, I) is sampled from a standard normal and then scaled/shifted by the learned μ and σ parameters. This makes the sampling operation differentiable.

**Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)**

VAEs are trained by maximizing the ELBO, a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood of the data. The ELBO combines reconstruction loss (how well the decoder reconstructs inputs) with KL divergence loss (how close the latent distribution stays to a prior, typically standard normal). Balancing these two terms controls the tradeoff between reconstruction quality and latent space structure.

**Latent Space Structure**

The latent space learned by VAEs has nice geometric properties. Interpolating between two points in latent space produces smooth transitions in the decoded outputs. This allows operations like "add feature A, subtract feature B" in latent space to meaningfully alter generated samples.

## How It Works

During training, VAEs process data through two stages. First, inputs are encoded into latent distribution parameters. Second, a sample is drawn from this distribution (using reparameterization), and the decoder reconstructs the input from this sample. The loss penalizes both reconstruction error (L2 or binary cross-entropy depending on data type) and deviation of the latent distribution from the prior.

The KL divergence term acts as a regularizer, pushing the learned distributions toward the standard normal prior. This prevents overfitting to specific training examples and encourages the latent space to be continuous and well-structured. At test time, new samples can be generated by sampling directly from the prior distribution and passing through the decoder.

VAEs can model arbitrary data distributions, though the assumed prior (typically Gaussian) and conditional distributions (decoder) limit expressiveness. More sophisticated variants like normalizing flows and inverse autoregressive flows increase the flexibility of the latent distribution.

## Practical Applications

**Image Generation and Manipulation**

VAEs generate new images by sampling from the latent space. The structured latent space enables semantic editing—changing specific dimensions affects attributes like color, shape, or expression. VAEs are used in image inpainting, where missing parts are filled by sampling from the learned distribution.

**Drug Discovery and Molecular Design**

VAEs encode molecules in continuous latent spaces where interpolation can lead to novel chemical structures. The decoder generates molecules from latent coordinates, enabling gradient-based optimization of molecular properties for drug candidates.

**Anomaly Detection**

The reconstruction error serves as an anomaly score—inputs that the VAE cannot reconstruct well are likely anomalous. Applications include fraud detection, industrial defect detection, and medical anomaly identification.

**Representation Learning**

VAEs learn disentangled representations where independent latent dimensions correspond to independent factors of variation (e.g., pose vs. lighting vs. color). This facilitates downstream tasks and improves interpretability.

## Examples

```python
# Simple VAE implementation in PyTorch
import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class VAE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim, latent_dim):
        super().__init__()
        
        # Encoder
        self.encoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(input_dim, 512),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(512, 256),
            nn.ReLU()
        )
        self.fc_mu = nn.Linear(256, latent_dim)
        self.fc_logvar = nn.Linear(256, latent_dim)
        
        # Decoder
        self.decoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(latent_dim, 256),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(256, 512),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(512, input_dim),
            nn.Sigmoid()
        )
    
    def reparameterize(self, mu, logvar):
        std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar)
        eps = torch.randn_like(std)
        return mu + eps * std
    
    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.encoder(x)
        mu = self.fc_mu(h)
        logvar = self.fc_logvar(h)
        z = self.reparameterize(mu, logvar)
        return self.decoder(z), mu, logvar

def vae_loss(recon_x, x, mu, logvar):
    # Reconstruction loss
    BCE = nn.functional.binary_cross_entropy(recon_x, x, reduction='sum')
    # KL divergence loss
    KLD = -0.5 * torch.sum(1 + logvar - mu.pow(2) - logvar.exp())
    return BCE + KLD
```

## Personal Notes

VAEs struck an elegant balance between tractability and expressiveness. The ELBO provides an interpretable training objective, though the reconstruction-KL tradeoff requires careful tuning. While diffusion models have surpassed VAEs in image quality for many tasks, VAEs remain valuable for their invertibility (useful in flow-based models) and for applications requiring meaningful latent arithmetic.
