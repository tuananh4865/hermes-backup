# Goals

## Overview

In project and product management, goals serve as the foundational statements that define what a team or organization aims to achieve. Goals provide direction, align efforts across stakeholders, and establish criteria for evaluating success. Without clear goals, teams risk working in fragmented directions, wasting resources on low-impact activities, and failing to deliver meaningful outcomes.

Goals in this context typically operate at multiple levels: strategic goals that span the entire organization, team-level objectives that support broader initiatives, and individual contributor goals that map to team deliverables. Effective goals connect day-to-day work to larger business outcomes, creating a clear line of sight between tasks and strategy.

Well-formed goals share several characteristics. They are specific enough to provide guidance on what needs to happen, measurable to track progress, achievable within resource constraints, relevant to broader organizational priorities, and time-bound with clear deadlines or milestones.

## Frameworks

### OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

OKRs, popularized by Intel and later adopted by Google, are a goal-setting framework that separates the qualitative objective from quantitative key results. An objective answers "where do we want to go?" while key results answer "how do we know we got there?"

OKRs are typically set quarterly, with organizations cascading them from company level to team and individual levels. A well-written objective is ambitious and inspirational, while key results are specific, measurable, and time-bound. OKR cycles include periodic check-ins and end-of-cycle reviews to assess completion and learn from results.

### SMART Goals

The SMART framework provides criteria for writing clear, actionable goals. Each letter represents a requirement:

- **Specific**: Goals should be clear and unambiguous, answering who, what, when, where, and why.
- **Measurable**: Include concrete criteria for measuring progress and completion.
- **Achievable**: Goals should stretch capabilities but remain realistic given available resources.
- **Relevant**: Goals must align with broader business objectives and priorities.
- **Time-bound**: Every goal needs a deadline or milestone to create urgency and focus.

## Tracking

Tracking goals requires consistent measurement against defined metrics. For OKRs, teams typically update key results weekly or bi-weekly, reviewing progress during standups or dedicated review sessions. Color-coded dashboards (green, yellow, red) help visualize status at a glance.

Effective tracking mechanisms include regular check-ins to identify blockers, milestone celebrations to maintain momentum, and retrospective analysis to understand variance between planned and actual outcomes. The key is balancing visibility with action—data collection should inform course corrections, not become bureaucratic overhead.

Goal tracking software ranges from dedicated platforms like Jira, Asana, and Workboard to spreadsheet-based systems. The tool matters less than the discipline of regular review and willingness to adjust based on evidence.
