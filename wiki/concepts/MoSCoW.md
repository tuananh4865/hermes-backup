# MoSCoW

## Overview

MoSCoW is a widely-used prioritization technique in project management, software development, and requirements engineering. The method provides a simple yet effective framework for categorizing items by their importance, enabling teams to make informed decisions about what to deliver first and what can be deferred. The acronym MoSCoW represents four distinct priority levels: Must have, Should have, Could have, and Won't have. Each category serves a specific purpose in balancing stakeholder needs against resource constraints, helping organizations focus their efforts on high-impact work while maintaining clarity about what falls outside the current scope.

The technique was popularized by Dai Clegg, a software developer and trainer who introduced the approach in the late 1990s as part of his work on dynamic systems development. Since then, MoSCoW has become a staple in agile methodologies, particularly within frameworks like [[Scrum]] and [[Kanban]], where iterative delivery and continuous prioritization are fundamental principles. Its intuitive naming convention makes it accessible to both technical and non-technical stakeholders, facilitating productive conversations about trade-offs and delivery expectations.

MoSCoW is especially valuable in time-boxed environments where teams must deliver a viable product within fixed deadlines and budgets. By clearly distinguishing between essential requirements and desirable but optional features, the method helps prevent scope creep while ensuring that critical business needs are met. When applied correctly, MoSCoW transforms vague discussions about priorities into structured, actionable agreements that guide development efforts throughout a project lifecycle.

## Categories

### Must Have

Must have requirements represent the non-negotiable elements of a project. These are the features, functionalities, or conditions that must be delivered for the product to be considered viable and accepted by stakeholders. Without these items, the deliverable fails to meet its fundamental purpose or cannot solve the problem it was designed to address. In practice, the Must have category should be kept lean; overpopulating it dilutes the meaning of the classification and undermines the technique's effectiveness. Common examples include core security features, regulatory compliance requirements, or basic functionality that defines the product's minimum viable form.

### Should Have

Should have requirements are important but not strictly essential for immediate delivery. These items have significant value and should be included if time and resources permit, but their absence does not compromise the product's core functionality or market viability. Should have items are often dependencies or enablers that enhance the user experience without being outright blockers. In many cases, teams may choose to defer should-have items to a subsequent release or sprint, treating them as high-priority candidates for future iteration.

### Could Have

Could have requirements describe features that would be nice to have but are considered optional luxuries. These items typically enhance user satisfaction, improve brand perception, or provide incremental value without materially affecting the product's primary function. Because of their lower priority, could-have items are often the first to be cut when project timelines shrink or scope adjustments become necessary. When resources are abundant, including could-have features can delight users and provide a competitive edge, but they should never come at the expense of must-have or should-have deliverables.

### Won't Have

Won't have requirements are items that the team has explicitly decided to exclude from the current delivery cycle. This category is not a placeholder for forgotten ideas; rather, it represents conscious decisions about what does not align with current objectives, budget constraints, or strategic direction. Documenting won't-have items prevents confusion and misaligned expectations among stakeholders, ensuring that everyone understands what is deliberately off the table for the time being. These items may be revisited in future planning phases if circumstances change.

## How to Apply

Applying MoSCoW prioritization begins with assembling the right stakeholders in a collaborative workshop or backlog refinement session. The goal is to evaluate each requirement, feature, or user story against the four categories through structured discussion rather than unilateral decision-making. Facilitators should guide conversations by asking probing questions such as: What happens if we do not deliver this? Would the product fail without it? Is this item legally or contractually required? This investigative approach ensures that classifications are grounded in rationale rather than assumption.

Once items are categorized, the team should validate the overall distribution. A healthy MoSCoW split for most projects follows an approximate ratio of 60% Must have, 20% Should have, 15% Could have, and 5% Won't have, though these percentages vary depending on context and complexity. If the Must have category becomes too large, the team risks overcommitting and diluting focus. Conversely, an overly aggressive Could have list may indicate unrealistic expectations about available resources.

After initial classification, MoSCoW priorities should be revisited regularly throughout the project lifecycle. As new information emerges, customer feedback arrives, or business conditions shift, some items may be reclassified to reflect updated realities. This iterative review process keeps the prioritization relevant and prevents outdated assumptions from constraining decision-making. Additionally, clear communication of MoSCoW classifications to all stakeholders, including sponsors, developers, and customers, ensures shared understanding and minimizes disputes during delivery.
